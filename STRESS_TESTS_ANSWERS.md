# GoNepal Brutal Stress Tests — Honest Answers

---

## Stress Test 1: The "So What" Test

**The One Thing:** GoNepal is the **only app that works completely offline with pre-downloaded trekking maps, real-time weather alerts for high-altitude routes, and emergency SOS contacts** — all bundled together. Google Maps doesn't work offline for Nepali trekking trails. TripAdvisor doesn't have offline trail maps. No other app combines this with the ability to message guides in offline mode.

**The catch:** This only matters when they're already on a trail without signal. The app has an awareness problem — tourists need to know GoNepal exists *before* they lose signal, which means the pre-trip funnel is critical (see Stress Test 5).

**Current reality:** If a tourist lands at TIA with working SIM and Google Maps, GoNepal is effectively invisible to them for the first 3-5 days. This is a serious problem.

---

## Stress Test 2: The Retention Cliff

**The honest answer:** There is no effective re-engagement mechanism for tourists who leave Nepal. This is a structural problem with tourism apps.

**What we can do:**
- Build a "memory" feature — auto-generate a beautifully designed trip journal from their saved places, photos, and itinerary that they can share and revisit
- Partner with travel photographers to offer "your trip in photos" — bring them back emotionally
- Create a "Nepal Alumni" community for past tourists to connect with those planning trips (indirect retention through social proof for future tourists)

**The CAC justification:** We accept that tourists are one-time users. The unit economics must work on a single trip. This means:
- Higher transaction commission per user (guides, experiences, domestic flights)
- B2B partnerships with travel agencies who embed GoNepal into their pre-trip packages
- The app is a customer acquisition tool for Nepali tourism businesses — they pay for leads, not for retention

**This isn't ideal.** But it's the reality of tourism. The goal is to make enough money per tourist trip to justify CAC.

## Stress Test 3: The Guide Marketplace Cold Start

**The exact sequence:**

**Phase 1: Supply before demand (Weeks 1-4)**
- Partner with the **Nepal Tourism Board** and **Trekking Agencies Association of Nepal (TAAN)** — get their existing member directories
- Offer the first 20 guides **free premium features** (verified badge, priority listing) for 6 months in exchange for joining
- Recruit through **Nepali tourism Facebook groups** — these are active and trusted communities
- Create a simplified paper-based signup that works for guides with basic smartphones

**Phase 2: Launch with supply (Week 5)**
- Launch the guide marketplace with 20+ verified guides, even if zero tourists are browsing
- Seed the marketplace with "sample" tourist profiles (not fake — create scenarios) to show how it works

**Phase 3: Demand activation (Weeks 6-10)**
- Partner with 3-5 Kathmandu-based travel agencies who will require guides to list on GoNepal (this creates demand pull-through)
- Run targeted Instagram/Facebook ads in source markets (India, China, UK, Australia) with the angle "Book verified local guides directly"
- Offer tourists a "first booking discount" funded by the travel agency partnerships

**The brutal truth:** Getting the first 20 guides is the hard part. After that, word-of-mouth from satisfied guides brings more guides. But this requires real, Grindr-style offline outreach (not "we'll post on social media"). I need to physically go to Kathmandu and meet guides face-to-face.

## Stress Test 4: The Nepali User Problem

**The pivot:** GoNepal must serve locals from day one. Ignoring 30 million Nepali people for 1.5 million tourists is a fatal flaw.

**What locals need:**
- **Local business directory** — Nepali people traveling within Nepal need restaurant/hotel reviews in Nepali
- **Domestic bus/flight booking** — locals book domestic travel too, and commission is the same whether foreign or Nepali
- **Event/ticket booking** — festivals, concerts, sports events
- **Local language support** — build the app in Nepali-first, not English-first
- **Community features** — local review forums, trip sharing in Nepali

**The expansion strategy:**
When expanding to Bhutan, Ladakh, Southeast Asia — we build for locals first in each market. The app becomes a regional domestic travel app, not just a tourist app. Locals in Bhutan need the same things — booking domestic flights, finding restaurants, navigating their own country.

**Word-of-mouth is the only scalable acquisition channel.** Without locals, every user costs $5-15 to acquire. With locals, organic growth reduces CAC to near zero.

## Stress Test 5: The Connectivity Paradox

**The pre-trip onboarding funnel:**

1. **Partner with airlines** — when tourists book flights to Nepal (through GoNepal or partners), show a "Prepare for Nepal" screen that prompts app download with their itinerary pre-loaded

2. **Airport welcome SMS** — partner with NTC/NCELL to send a welcome SMS on landing: "Welcome to Nepal. Download GoNepal for offline trekking maps and emergency contacts. [download link]"

3. **Hotel integration** — partner with 20 Kathmandu hotels to include a QR code in room welcome packets that auto-downloads the app with the hotel's location pre-cached

4. **In-app prompts** — when users open the app in Kathmandu (detected via GPS or manual selection), show a clear prompt: "You're in Kathmandu. Tap to download offline maps for your trek." One-tap cache for popular routes (Annapurna, Everest, Langtang)

5. **Pre-trip email sequence** — for users who sign up before arriving, send emails: "3 days until Nepal — download your offline maps now"

**The metric:** Track "pre-cached users" as a key metric. Target: 40% of users who book treks should have routes cached before leaving Kathmandu.

## Stress Test 6: The Revenue Reality

**At 10,000 MAU:**

- **Monthly active users:** 10,000 (this includes all users who opened the app in the last 30 days)
- **Trip users (in Nepal right now):** ~1,500 (based on seasonality)
- **Domestic flight bookers:** Maybe 2-3% of trip users = 30-45 bookings/month
- **Guide hires:** Maybe 5-8% of trip users = 75-120 hires/month
- **Restaurant/activity affiliates:** 10% of trip users = 150 transactions

**Revenue calculation:**
- Flight commission: 45 x $1.80 = $81/month
- Guide commission: 100 x $15 average = $1,500/month
- Restaurant/activities: 150 x $3 average = $450/month

**Total MRR at 10K MAU: ~$2,000/month**

This is brutally low. The math doesn't work at 10K MAU.

**What needs to happen:**
- 10K MAU is too small. We need 100K+ MAU for meaningful revenue
- B2B is the answer: Travel agencies pay for leads. At $5-10 per qualified lead, even 200 leads/month = $2,000/month additional revenue
- Premium features for guides: Verified badge, analytics dashboard — $10/month x 200 guides = $2,000/month

**Honest MRR target at 10K MAU: $4,000-5,000/month** — still not sustainable. We need to reach 50K MAU before the business model works.

## Stress Test 7: The Competitor Who Actually Has Money

**What MakeMyTrip cannot easily replicate:**

1. **Offline-first architecture built specifically for Himalayan conditions** — this requires 2+ years of iteration, real user feedback, and understanding of the specific failure modes. MakeMyTrip's core product is online-first. Rewriting for offline is a massive undertaking.

2. **Local guide relationships** — we've spent time building these. Guides trust us. MakeMyTrip is seen as an Indian company in Nepal — there's local resistance.

3. **Nepali-language-first positioning** — MakeMyTrip targets English-speaking users. Going Nepali-first is a strategic pivot they won't make because their investors won't understand it.

4. **Government partnerships** — Nepal Tourism Board relationship. This takes time and trust. You can't buy it with a $50M marketing budget.

5. **The data moat** — User behavior data, offline map corrections, trail difficulty ratings, weather patterns — this compounds over time. Every user who caches a trail generates data that improves the product.

**The honest answer:** The moat is thin. If MakeMyTrip commits serious resources, they could catch up in 18-24 months. Our only real defense is speed — get to 100K users and meaningful revenue before they notice, then build enough local depth that switching costs are high.

## Stress Test 8: The Team Bus Factor

**Current reality:** Bus factor = 1. The company is me. Every partnership, line of code, system design, and business relationship lives in my head.

**Can GoNepal survive without me for 6 months right now?** Absolutely not.

**What needs to change:**

1. **Documentation:** Every system, partnership, and process must be documented in a structured way that a replacement could understand

2. **No single points of failure:** 
   - Partnership relationships should involve at least 2 people on our side
   - Key code modules should be reviewable by others
   - Bank accounts, domains, and legal entities should have succession plans

3. **Revenue before redundancy:** We need to reach $10K MRR before we can afford to hire even one part-time person. Until then, I'm doing everything.

4. **The eventual team:**
   - First hire: Part-time Nepali community manager (can be remote, $300-500/month)
   - Second hire: Part-time developer for bug fixes and maintenance ($500-800/month)
   - These are sustainable at $8-10K MRR

**The uncomfortable truth:** GoNepal cannot survive without me today. The goal is to build it to a point where it *could* survive — but that requires reaching $20K+ MRR first, which requires solving all the other stress tests first.

## CRITICAL CORRECTIONS (After Feedback)

I buried this in the middle of a section. Let me be clear:

**Consumer revenue at current scale is a rounding error.** At 10K MAU, the $2,000 MRR from commission won't cover my coffee. 

**The real business model is B2B:**
- **Travel agencies** pay $5-10 per qualified lead (tourist who books a trek)
- **Hotels** pay for integration into GoNepal's "Book Your Stay" feature
- **Airlines** (Yeti, Buddha, Tara) pay for preferential listing and booking widget
- **Guides** pay $10/month for premium features (analytics, verified badge, priority)

**Time allocation:** I should spend 60% of my time on B2B sales, 30% on product, 10% on marketing. Currently I have this backwards.
